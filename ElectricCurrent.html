Q1 Which of the following equation shows the correct relationship between electrical units? ‚ñº
üí° Solution

The correct relationship between electrical units is:

1 A = 1 C/s

Explanation: This equation defines electric current (Ampere) as the rate of flow of electric charge (Coulomb) per unit time (second).

The relationship shows that 1 Ampere equals 1 Coulomb per second, which is the fundamental definition of electric current.

Q2 By what name is the physical quantity coulomb/second called? ‚ñº
üí° Solution

The physical quantity coulomb/second is called Ampere.

Coulomb/Second = Ampere (A)

Key Point: Ampere is the SI unit of electric current, representing the flow of one coulomb of charge per second.

Q3 What is the flow of charge called? ‚ñº
üí° Solution

The flow of charge is called Electric Current.

Electric Current = Flow of Electric Charge

Definition: Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charges through a conductor or any medium.

It is measured in Amperes (A) and represents how much charge flows past a point in a circuit per unit time.

Q4 What travels through the wires when you switch on a light? ‚ñº
üí° Solution

When you switch on a light, Electrons travel through the wires.

Electric Current in Wires = Flow of Electrons

Explanation: In metallic conductors (like copper wires), electric current is carried by the movement of free electrons from the negative terminal to the positive terminal of the power source.

These electrons carry electrical energy that powers the light bulb.

Q5 Which particles constitute the electric current in a metallic conductor? ‚ñº
üí° Solution

In a metallic conductor, Electrons constitute the electric current.

Metallic Conductor Current = Electron Flow

Key Points:

  • Metals have free electrons that can move easily through the crystal lattice
  • These free electrons carry negative charge
  • When a potential difference is applied, electrons drift from negative to positive terminal
  • This drift of electrons constitutes electric current in metals
Q6 In which direction does conventional current flow around a circuit? In which direction do electrons flow? ‚ñº
üí° Solution

(a) Conventional Current Direction:

Conventional current flows from the positive terminal of a battery to the negative terminal through the external circuit.

(b) Electron Flow Direction:

Electrons flow from the negative terminal to the positive terminal of the battery (opposite to conventional current).

Conventional Current: (+) ‚Üí (-)
Electron Flow: (-) ‚Üí (+)

Important Note: Conventional current direction was established before the discovery of electrons. Though electrons actually flow in the opposite direction, we still use conventional current direction for circuit analysis.

Q7 What is the unit of electric current? ‚ñº
üí° Solution

The unit of electric current is Ampere (symbol: A).

Electric Current Unit = Ampere (A)

Definition: One ampere is the current that flows when one coulomb of charge passes through any cross-section of a conductor in one second.

Ampere is one of the seven fundamental SI base units.

Q12 If 10 C of charge pass a point in a circuit in 1 s, what current is flowing? ‚ñº
üí° Solution

Given:

  • Charge (Q) = 10 C
  • Time (t) = 1 s
  • Current (I) = ?
Formula: I = Q/t
Calculation:
I = Q/t
I = 10 C / 1 s
I = 10 A

Answer: The current flowing is 10 Amperes.

Q16 What is an electric current? What makes an electric current flow in a wire? Define the unit of electric current (or Define ampere). ‚ñº
üí° Solution

(a) Electric Current:

An electric current is a flow of electric charges (electrons) through a conductor.

(b) What makes current flow:

Potential difference between the ends of the wire makes electric current flow in the wire.

(c) Definition of Ampere:

When 1 coulomb of charge flows through any cross-section of a conductor in 1 second, the electric current flowing through it is said to be 1 ampere.

1 Ampere = 1 Coulomb / 1 Second
1 A = 1 C/s

Key Points:

  • Current flows due to potential difference
  • Ampere is the SI unit of current
  • Current is rate of charge flow
